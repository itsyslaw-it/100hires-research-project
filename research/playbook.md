# Cold Outreach Pipeline for B2B SaaS - Playbook

## 1. Overview

This playbook outlines a practical cold outreach system for B2B SaaS companies, based on insights from active practitioners in outbound sales. It is designed for early-stage teams or individuals looking to generate pipeline through cold email and LinkedIn outreach.

---

## 2. Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Pricing Strategy: Outcome-based Premium Pricing vs Upfront Price Transparency

- Alex Berman argue on his 4 minutes video for aggressive, outcome based pricing. In the transcript collected in this repo, he says: "If your price feels totally fair to the client, you've already lost money." He also argues that sellers should price against client outcomes, not just internal cost.  
(source: Alex Berman, YouTube, 14 April 2026 - [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kCIz_-JSAJw](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kCIz_-JSAJw))
- Josh Braun basically mean is don’t avoid or “hide” your price when a prospect asks, just be honest and upfront. The idea is that buyers can’t decide if something is worth it without knowing how much it costs, so dodging the question only creates distrust and wastes time. Instead, give a rough price range early, then watch their reaction to see if they’re a good fit. If theyre okay with it, you move forward; if not, you both save time. In short, being transparent about pricing helps you build trust faster and focus only on prospects who can actually buy.  
(source: Josh Braun, LinkedIn, February 2022 - [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_whats-the-price-how-do-you-respond-activity-6898642550232547329-YcpO](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_whats-the-price-how-do-you-respond-activity-6898642550232547329-YcpO))

My decision:  
I choose Alex Berman because he is stronger on pricing design, but Josh Braun is stronger on pricing communication. From my prespective is the best approach is to set pricing based on outcomes and premium value, then communicate that price transparently once the buyer asks.

### 2. Activity Scale vs Message Quality

- Kyle Coleman highlights a large activity lift, from 35 calls per rep per day to 150, driven by infrastructure, tooling, and process design.  
(source: Kyle Coleman, LinkedIn, 2026 - [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kyletcoleman_same-team-size-3x-the-pipeline-im-incredibly-activity-7424877366570610688-opQa](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kyletcoleman_same-team-size-3x-the-pipeline-im-incredibly-activity-7424877366570610688-opQa))
- Nick Abraham argues against pure high-volume cold calling focused on speed and booking quantity. In his post, he shares how his team booked many meetings through high activity but ended up with a 30% no-show rate and fake pipeline because reps skipped proper qualification to hit numbers. He recommends focusing on real qualification (timeline, budget, decision-makers, current solutions) during calls instead of “script speed-runs,” resulting in better-qualified meetings and real opportunities rather than inflated activity metrics.
(source: Nick Abraham, LinkedIn, 2026 - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nick-abraham_we-hired-cold-callers-and-thought-everything-activity-7391549808500498432-S0sS)

My decision:  
I side more with Josh Braun on sequencing. Activity and systems matter, but weak messaging quality compounds wasted effort. Getting attention is the first and most critical step, if your opening message isn’t clear, relevant, and interesting enough, people won’t engage at all, especially with tools like call screening. Kyle’s approach of scaling activity and systems only works after you already have messaging that resonates, but without that, more calls and better tools just amplify failure. So it’s smarter to fix the message first, then scale it.

### 3. Signal-as-message vs Signal-as-timing Trigger

- Nick Abraham treats a strong signal as something you can reference directly in outreach. In his public LinkedIn post, he recommends monitoring freelance job boards and then reaching out by referencing the post itself. His exact framing is that this kind of signal means urgent demand, and that "There likely isn't a bigger signal than that."  
(source: Nick Abraham, LinkedIn, 2026 - [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nick-abraham_everyone-in-outbound-wants-to-talk-about-activity-7426981739362693120-9TPV](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nick-abraham_everyone-in-outbound-wants-to-talk-about-activity-7426981739362693120-9TPV))
- Florin Tatulea explicitly pushes back on this style of signal-led messaging. In his public LinkedIn post, he writes: "The signal should NOT be your message." He argues that the signal only suggests timing, while the seller still has to add context and value.  
(source: Florin Tatulea, LinkedIn, 2026 - [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/florintatulea_outbound-sales-activity-7416912298587561984-b2dJ](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/florintatulea_outbound-sales-activity-7416912298587561984-b2dJ))

My decision:  
I side with Florin Tatulea. Nick is right that strong signals improve timing, but Florin's distinction is more durable: a signal is evidence of possible interest, not the value proposition itself. The safest rule is to use the signal for timing and research, then write the message around context and business relevance.

### 4. Deep Personalization vs Scalable Relevance

- In Becc Holland video's (13:00-32:00), she's argue that modern outbound sales should shift from generic, high-volume tactics to deep, insight-driven personalization and authentic, human-centered messaging. In “The 2025 Outbound Playbook,” she emphasizes that buyers are overwhelmed by low-value outreach, so sellers must stand out by delivering unique insights, unrecognized problems, context, data, and genuine authenticity to achieve much higher response rates.
(source: Becc Holland, Youtube, 24 July 2025 - [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=minPll66uek](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=minPll66uek))
- Alex Berman point of view contradicts Becca's method in one of his LinkedIn post. He argues that heavy personalization, especially referencing minor personal details has become overused, unnatural, and often counterproductive, sometimes even triggering spam filters. Instead, he recommends a “one-to-one, to many” approach: writing simple, human, role-relevant messages using broader business signals that can still be sent at scale.  
(source: Alex Berman, LinkedIn, March 2025 - [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexanderberman_is-the-personal-compliment-dead-in-2025-activity-7320457607926673408-Lbxc/](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexanderberman_is-the-personal-compliment-dead-in-2025-activity-7320457607926673408-Lbxc/))

My decision:  
I side with Alex Berman. While Becc Holland’s approach can be powerful in highly targeted, high value deals, Alex’s framework is more practical and scalable for most outbound teams. His “one-to-one, to many” method reduces execution complexity, avoids forced personalization, and allows teams to maintain both consistency and volume while still staying relevant. This is also what I do to reach potential client in my current role as a B2B salesman, but unique insight and high value outreach is also important.

## 3. What I rejected and Why
After reviewing the experts, I decided against two popular ideas:

- Pure high-volume cold calling without strong qualification (Kyle Coleman’s infrastructure driven activity lift from 35 → 150 calls/day).

While the tooling and process improvements are impressive, I reject blindly scaling activity before fixing messaging and qualification. As Nick Abraham showed, high activity without proper qualification creates fake pipeline and high no-show rates. (Source: Kyle Coleman, LinkedIn 2026; Nick Abraham, LinkedIn 2026)
- Heavy personalized messsaging that references minor personal details (Becc Holland’s deep insight-driven personalization).

I rejected making this the default approach. In most cases it feels forced, is hard to scale, increases execution complexity, and can trigger spam filters. It works well for very high-value, low-volume deals, but not for consistent team execution. (Source: Becc Holland, YouTube “The 2025 Outbound Playbook”, 24 July 2025; Alex Berman, LinkedIn March 2025)

## 6. Who I Would NOT Recommend Following and Why

Out of all the experts I studied, there are two I wouldn’t recommend as your main mentors:

### 1. Kyle Coleman (High-Volume Activity Focus)
His numbers look great on paper, but I wouldn’t follow this approach as the main strategy. Prioritizing “more calls” before fixing messaging and qualification is risky and can create a lot of low-quality or “fake” pipeline. In my current role as a B2B salesman, I tried pushing high-volume outreach, and it ended up being exhausting with only minor results. Quality and proper qualification should come first.

### 2. Becc Holland (Extreme Deep Personalization)
Her thinking is very smart and buyer-focused, but it’s too time-consuming and difficult to scale for most teams. Relying heavily on this approach often leads to low output and messages that can feel forced or overly crafted.

---

### Note
The wisest approach is to balance both methods, combine smart, efficient volume with clear messaging and just the right amount of personalization. Going all-in on either extreme usually creates more problems than it solves. I still respect both of them and believe they offer valuable insights in the right situations. However, they are not the most practical default frameworks for early-stage or mid-sized outbound teams.
