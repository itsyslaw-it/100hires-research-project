# Cold Outreach Pipeline for B2B SaaS - Playbook

## 1. Overview
This playbook outlines a practical cold outreach system for B2B SaaS companies, based on insights from active practitioners in outbound sales. It is designed for early-stage teams or individuals looking to generate pipeline through cold email and LinkedIn outreach.

---
## 2. Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Pricing Strategy: Outcome-based Premium Pricing vs Upfront Price Transparency

- Alex Berman argue on his 4 minutes video for aggressive, outcome based pricing. In the transcript collected in this repo, he says: "If your price feels totally fair to the client, you've already lost money." He also argues that sellers should price against client outcomes, not just internal cost.  
(source: Alex Berman, YouTube, 14 April 2026 - https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kCIz_-JSAJw)

- Josh Braun basically mean is don’t avoid or “hide” your price when a prospect asks, just be honest and upfront. The idea is that buyers can’t decide if something is worth it without knowing how much it costs, so dodging the question only creates distrust and wastes time. Instead, give a rough price range early, then watch their reaction to see if they’re a good fit. If theyre okay with it, you move forward; if not, you both save time. In short, being transparent about pricing helps you build trust faster and focus only on prospects who can actually buy.  
(source: Josh Braun, LinkedIn, February 2022 - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_whats-the-price-how-do-you-respond-activity-6898642550232547329-YcpO)

My decision:  
I choose Alex Berman because he is stronger on pricing design, but Josh Braun is stronger on pricing communication. From my prespective is the best approach is to set pricing based on outcomes and premium value, then communicate that price transparently once the buyer asks.

### 2. Activity Scale vs Message Quality

- Kyle Coleman highlights a large activity lift, from 35 calls per rep per day to 150, driven by infrastructure, tooling, and process design.  
(source: Kyle Coleman, LinkedIn, 2026 - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kyletcoleman_same-team-size-3x-the-pipeline-im-incredibly-activity-7424877366570610688-opQa)

- Josh Braun argues outreach effectiveness starts with clarity, relevance, and empathy in the opener, including transcript-first contexts.  
(source: Josh Braun, LinkedIn, 2026 - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_ios-26-just-made-cold-calling-better-not-activity-7338632537465896961-MhIW)

My decision:  
I side more with Josh Braun on sequencing. Activity and systems matter, but weak messaging quality compounds wasted effort. Getting attention is the first and most critical step, if your opening message isn’t clear, relevant, and interesting enough, people won’t engage at all, especially with tools like call screening. Kyle’s approach of scaling activity and systems only works after you already have messaging that resonates, but without that, more calls and better tools just amplify failure. So it’s smarter to fix the message first, then scale it.

### 3. Signal-as-message vs Signal-as-timing Trigger

- Nick Abraham treats a strong signal as something you can reference directly in outreach. In his public LinkedIn post, he recommends monitoring freelance job boards and then reaching out by referencing the post itself. His exact framing is that this kind of signal means urgent demand, and that "There likely isn't a bigger signal than that."  
(source: Nick Abraham, LinkedIn, 2026 - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nick-abraham_everyone-in-outbound-wants-to-talk-about-activity-7426981739362693120-9TPV)

- Florin Tatulea explicitly pushes back on this style of signal-led messaging. In his public LinkedIn post, he writes: "The signal should NOT be your message." He argues that the signal only suggests timing, while the seller still has to add context and value.  
(source: Florin Tatulea, LinkedIn, 2026 - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/florintatulea_outbound-sales-activity-7416912298587561984-b2dJ)

My decision:  
I side with Florin Tatulea. Nick is right that strong signals improve timing, but Florin's distinction is more durable: a signal is evidence of possible interest, not the value proposition itself. The safest rule is to use the signal for timing and research, then write the message around context and business relevance.
